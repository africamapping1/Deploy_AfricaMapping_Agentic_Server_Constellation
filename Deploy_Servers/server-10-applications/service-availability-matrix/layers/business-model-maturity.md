# Business Model Maturity

## Purpose

This layer defines how the platform should evaluate the business-model depth of each tenant business.

It exists so the system can distinguish between a tenant that has only a high-level business shell and a tenant that has a more structured economic and operational model.

This is the business-model layer of the Service Availability Matrix.

## Why This Exists

A tenant may already have:

- a business README
- a canvas overview
- a communications layer
- a strategy layer

and still be weak in business-model depth.

That means the tenant may be explainable at a high level, but still underdefined in terms of how it creates, delivers, and sustains value.

This layer checks whether the tenant has begun to define the structural logic of the business itself.

## Core Principle

Business-model maturity is about structural depth.

It asks whether the tenant can explain not just what it says, but how the business actually works.

## Minimum Business Model Baseline

At this time, each tenant should at least have:

### 1. Business Model README
The tenant should define its business-model layer.

This is the minimum entry point for structural business thinking.

## Expanded Business Model Maturity

A more mature business model layer may include files such as:

- customer-segments.md
- value-proposition.md
- channels.md
- customer-relationships.md
- revenue-model.md or revenue-or-funding.md
- key-resources.md
- key-activities.md
- key-partners.md
- cost-structure.md

These correspond to the structural business-model logic that should eventually be present for a mature tenant business.

## Maturity Logic

Useful maturity states for business-model items may include:

- missing
- placeholder
- scaffolded
- documented
- structured
- reusable

This helps the platform distinguish between a declared business-model area and a more complete structural business model.

## Comparison Rule

This layer should support questions such as:

- does the tenant only have a business-model README, or deeper model detail
- which model components are already expressed
- where is the tenant structurally weak
- where are multiple tenants starting to converge on comparable business-model depth

## Reproducibility Logic

If multiple tenants repeatedly express the same business-model components, those components may become part of the standard tenant business baseline.

Examples may include:

- value proposition
- customer or stakeholder segments
- key activities
- key resources
- key partners

This helps the platform promote repeated practice into stronger common structure.

## Audience and Tenant-Type Rule

Not every tenant will express business-model depth in identical language.

For example:

- one tenant may use customer language
- another may use stakeholder language
- one may use revenue model
- another may use revenue or funding logic

That is acceptable.

The matrix should care more about comparable structural meaning than strict wording uniformity.

## Governance Value

A tenant with stronger business-model maturity is easier to:

- compare
- communicate honestly
- reason about strategically
- support through the business-agent
- evaluate for parity and reproducibility
- scale into future registration patterns

Business-model maturity is therefore both a business and governance concern.

## Relationship to Other Layers

This layer should stay distinct from:

- communications maturity
- canvas maturity
- surface availability

The business model defines structural truth.

The communications layer explains that truth.

The canvas gives the whole-business picture.

The surface layer describes where the business appears operationally.

## Final Principle

A tenant business becomes more mature when it can explain its structure, not only its story.

This layer exists to make that structural maturity visible.
